## Phile 25. Dark web threat intelligence

This is the last episode of the first part of the series. We conclude it with an exciting topic: the dark web. Today let's explore how security professionals can gather threat intelligence from the internet's hidden places.


### What is dark web

Internet can be divided into 3 layers: surface web, deep web and dark web. 
**Surface web** is the publicly accessible part of the internet that search ewngines index. Users can browse websites, search for information and access open-source content through standard web browers. 
**Deep web** consists of content that is not indexed by search engines and requires authentication or authorization to access. This includes corporate intranets, private social media profiles, subscription-based services and internal databases. 
**Dark web** is the small part of deep web. It consists of hidden services that is accessible only through specialized software such as the Tor browser. It offers anonimity both for users and seervice operators. While the dark web has legitimate use-cases, it makes a popular environment for cybercriminal activities including the sale of stolen credentials, malware, illicit goods, and underground services.


 ### Dark web monitoring
Dark web monitoring plays a crucial role in proactive security operations. Understanding how underground communities operate allows analysts to identify emerging threats before they reach their targets.

Some of the most common reasons professionals monitor the dark web:

- Detecting stolen credentials or data leaks before public exposure
- Learning about newly discovered vulnerabilities, attack tools and malwares
- Identifying brand impersonation or discussions targeting the organization.
- Identifying emerging threat actors, campaigns

There are the two main methods to dark web monitoring:passive and active monitoring. 

#### Passive monitoring
Passsive monitoring involves collecting information without interacting with the community such as:
- Reading dark web forums
- Regularly visiting marketplaces and leak sites
- Using OSINT tools
- Subscribing breach notification and dark web monitoring services offered by security vendors

The advantage of passive monitoring is that it requires less preparation, presents lower operational risk and can be automated.
However the collected information is often  limited to publicly observable information.

#### Active monitoring
Active monitoring involves directly engaging with underground communities. This may include creating fake personas, participating in conversations,communicating with forum members or interacting with marketplace administrators. 
The main advantage is gaining deeper and more timely intelligence that is not available only through passive collection. 
However active monitoring requires advanced technical expertise, strict operational security, isolated envoronments and understanding the legal implications

### Legal considerations
Many activties and content on the dark web are illegal. During dark web investigations - especially when conducting active monitoring - it is essential to understand the applicable law and strictly operate within legal boundaries.
If there is any uncertainty, consult legal counsel before engaging with threat actors or underground communities. Organizations should establish clear procedures, obtain the necessary approvals, and document investigative activities to ensure legal and ethical compliance.

### Final takeaways

The dark web is one of the richest sources of cyber threat intelligence, but it is also one of the most challenging environments to investigate. Effective intelligence collection requires much more than technical skills—it demands careful planning, legal awareness, and disciplined operational security.
