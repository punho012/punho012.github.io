### Phile 10. Stay safe, stay aware, there are adversaries out there!

In the last five chapters, we explored several CTI frameworks. Today we move forward and start discussing the adversaries themselves.
The threat landscape is constantly evolving — not only in terms of techniques and tools, but also the attackers behind them. New hacking groups emerge every day, searching for weaknesses, attempting to evade detection, and working to achieve their objectives. 
Objectives are a key factor in the story. Understanding a threat actor’s goals is essential. It not only helps strengthen our defenses but also provides a useful perspective for categorizing different types of attackers.
Gaining information about hacking groups operating in the wild is crucial from a defensive point of view. Organizations must design their defensive infrastructure based on the specific threats they face.

So let’s take a look at who we are up against.

**Cybercriminals**

Their primary objective is financial gain. Profit is their main motivation. They develop malware such as ransomware and credential stealers, search for unpatched systems, or attempt to gain unauthorized access to networks.
They often sell their services and products on dark web forums, darknet marketplaces, and Telegram channels. Cybercriminal operations tend to move quickly — once they achieve their objective, they usually move on to the next target.

**Script-kiddies**

Script kiddies are essentially beginners in the hacking world. They typically have basic knowledge of computers and networking but lack deeper technical skills, especially in programming and exploit development.

Instead of creating their own tools, they rely on publicly available scripts or software developed by others. Because of this limitation, they usually cannot conduct complex or highly sophisticated attacks on their own.

**Hactivists**

Hacktivists operate based on ideology or political motivations. The infamous Anonymous collective is a well-known example.

Their goal is not financial profit but rather to promote or defend a particular political or social cause. Their activity often increases during geopolitical crises such as wars or political conflicts.

Hacktivist groups commonly conduct DDoS attacks, website defacement, and sometimes deploy destructive malware such as wipers.

**APT (advanced persisten threats)**

Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) groups are among the most sophisticated and dangerous types of threat actors. They are typically state-sponsored and operate in support of national interests.

These groups have significant financial resources, advanced technical capabilities, and long-term strategic goals. They frequently conduct cyber espionage campaigns and actively search for zero-day vulnerabilities.

Unlike many other attackers, APT groups are patient. Once they gain access to a network, they may remain undetected for months or even years. Their operations can target high-value intelligence, influence political processes, support military operations, or destabilize countries.

**Final takeaway**

Understanding who the attackers are is just as important as understanding how they operate. Each type of threat actor has different motivations, resources, and tactics. Recognizing these differences helps defenders prioritize threats and build more effective security strategies.
