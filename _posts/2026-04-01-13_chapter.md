### Phile 13. Attribution in CTI: Why It’s Hard — and Why It Often Goes Wrong

Attribution is one of the most challenging aspects of cyber threat intelligence. On the surface, it seems simple: identify who is behind an attack. In reality, it is rarely that straightforward.

Attackers actively try to hide their identity. They use VPNs, proxy infrastructure, compromised servers, and false flags to mislead investigators. A single operation may involve infrastructure spread across multiple countries, making it difficult to determine the true origin. On top of that, threat actors often reuse tools, techniques, and even code from other groups, intentionally or unintentionally blurring the lines between them.

Another challenge is visibility. No single organization has a complete picture of an attack. Each vendor or analyst sees only a fragment of the activity, which can lead to different conclusions. What looks like one group from one perspective may appear as something entirely different from another.

Attribution also involves a level of interpretation. Analysts connect indicators, behaviors, and context to form a hypothesis. However, without strong evidence, these assessments can be biased or premature. In some cases, geopolitical assumptions or media pressure can push organizations toward quick but inaccurate conclusions.

This is why attribution often goes wrong. It is not just a technical problem — it is an analytical one.

For defenders, the goal should not always be perfect attribution. Instead, the focus should be on understanding the adversary’s behavior, capabilities, and intent. Even without knowing exactly who is behind an attack, defenders can still detect, respond, and improve their security posture.

In CTI, attribution is valuable — but it should always be handled with caution.
