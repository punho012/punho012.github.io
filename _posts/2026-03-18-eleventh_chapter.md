### Phile 11. Hacktivists or State Actors? The Truth About Proxy Cyber Groups

After discussing the motivations behind hacking and cybercrime, and how different hacking communities can be categorized, now we take a look at a special type of group. These actors represent a mix of hacktivists and APTs — they are known as proxies. 
As discussed earlier, hacktivists operate based on ideology and political motivations, often conducting DDOS attacks or website defacement. However not all hakctivists operate independently. In some cases governments outsource certain operations and collaborate with these groups based on mutual interests. 

On the surface, these groups may appear to act independently but behind the scenes they are supported by nation-states. This relathionship offers advantages for both sides:

- Governments can carry out attack against their opponents while they can claim - they were activists.
- Hacktivist groups can gain reputation and financial or operational support.

Proxy groups often emerge during conflicts and geopolitical tensions. They are a powerful tool for governments and regimes to support broader strategic and military objectives.

Historically and recently this pattern is clearly visible. For example, during geopolitical escalations, several “independent” hacking groups have publicly threatened and carried out cyber attacks against organizations aligned with opposing sides. Similar tactics have been observed in conflicts such as the Russia–Ukraine war or US-Israel attack on Iran where both sides leveraged proxy actors to conduct cyber operations.
Targets often include defense contractors, government-related entities and critical infrastructure. Unfortunately, these attacks may impact civilians or in some cases, even risks life - for example when healtcare systems or hospitals are targeted. 

Altough it is challenging to determine whether a hactivist group is linked to a government, there are indicators that can help analysts. Infrastructure overlap, reuse of C2 frameworks, and similarities in tooling, codes can point toward deeper connection.
Timing is also a strong indicator. If an attack occurs shortly after a major geopolitical event, it may suggest nation-state involvement rather than purely independent activity.


### Final Takeaway

For defenders, understanding these subtle connections is critical. Recognizing patterns such as timing, infrastructure reuse, and geopolitical alignment can reveal what is happening beneath the surface.

In today’s threat landscape, not every “independent” attacker is truly independent — and that distinction can change how we assess risk, respond to incidents, and understand the bigger picture.
